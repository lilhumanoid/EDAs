Why was the data set created?

Purpose

  • Compare estimates of effects of worker exposure to environmental heat across different locations
  • Inform decision-making related to occupational heat injury and illness prevention planning

How was the data set created?

Derivation of measure

Measures 1-3 are derived from the U.S. Department of Labor's Bureau of Labor Statistics Survey of Occupational Injuries and Illnesses. The survey provides annual information on the incidence rate and number of work-related injuries, illnesses, and fatal injuries, and how these statistics vary by incident, industry, geography, occupation, and other characteristics. These data include measures relating to exposure to environmental heat.

Measure 4 is derived from the U.S. Department of Labor's Bureau of Labor Statistics Survey of Occupational Injuries and Illnesses by multiplying the annual median worker days lost due to exposure to environmental heat by injury and illness cases due to exposure to environmental heat.

What are the limitations of the data set?

  • The Survey of Occupational Injuries and Illnesses is an establishment-based survey used to estimate incidence rates and counts of workplace injuries and illnesses.
  • Not all states have available data. Participation is voluntary. State law dictates whether establishments must participate.
  • Only certain industries with more than 10 employees are required to keep the records.
  • Volunteers are not always included.
  • Federal employees are not included.
  • Counts less than 15 are suppressed.
  • The survey is largely recognized to undercount many types of injuries and illnesses, but the nature of undercounting is unclear.

How should these data be used?

Use Constraints

The Survey of Occupational Injuries and Illnesses data has several limitations:

  • It is an establishment-based survey used to estimate incidence rates and counts of workplace injuries and illnesses.
  • Not all states have available data; survey participation is voluntary.
  • Only certain industries with more than 10 employees are required to keep the records.
  • Counts less than 15 are suppressed.
  • The survey is largely recognized to undercount many types of injuries and illnesses, but the nature of undercounting is unclear; there is ongoing data quality work at the Bureau of Labor Statistics.
