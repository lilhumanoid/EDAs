Data details

Footnotes

  1. The emergency department visits data shown here are provided by state and/or local public health departments to the National Environmental Public Health Tracking Program.

  2. Emergency department visit records are selected using primary and other diagnosis codes and emergency department visit date between May 1 and September 30 for each year.

  3. These data represent number of emergency department visits rather than number of individuals. For example, a person visting the emergency department twice in one year would count as two visits.

  4. Data from Veterans Affairs, Indian Health Services and institutionalized (e.g., prison) populations are not included in the dataset submitted to National Environmental Public Health Tracking Program.

  5. Emergency department visits of residents to out-of-state hospitals are excluded for most states. Visits for border counties may be under estimated. The inclusion of these visits by some states can make affect state to state compariability.

  6. On October 1, 2015 in the US, ICD-10-CM replaced ICD-9-CM for coding of medical terminology and disease classification. This coding change impacts information classifications for hospital discharge, emergency department, and outpatient records for administrative and financial transactions in all healthcare settings. In 2015, data were coded as ICD-9-CM from January to September and as ICD-10-CM from October to December in 2015. Differences in counts and rates of heat stress illness in years prior to 2016 (ICD-9-CM) compared with 2016 and subsequent years (ICD-10-CM) could be a result of this coding change and not an actual difference in the number of events.

  7. Differences in the data between states or counties or over time may be due to the differences in coding, diagnostic techniques, and hospital admission practices.

  8. CDC Tracking Network suppresses data for counties with non-zero counts less than 6 and population less than 100,000 to protect confidentiality.

Why was the data set created?

Purpose

Measures 1-3:

Heat-related illness can manifest in several clinical outcomes, and people with chronic health problems (e.g., cardiovascular disease, diabetes, obesity) are more susceptible to the effects of heat than healthy individuals. For these reasons, heat-related illness may not be listed as the primary diagnosis. These measures, therefore, include all cases where heat-related illness is explicitly listed as the primary diagnosis or any other diagnosis.

Increases in the rates of ED visits for heat-related illness are one potential impact of rising global temperatures. Tracking these data can help document changes over place and time, monitor vulnerable areas, and evaluate the results of local climate-adaptation strategies.

Measures 4-5:

ED visits associated with HRI were identified using a combination of keywords based on reported symptoms, physician assessments, and discharge diagnoses indicating exposure to high ambient temperature. These near real-time data can be used by public health agencies to plan for and communicate the risk of HRI as ambient temperature increases. Potential uses include

  • Assessing the severity of an ongoing heat event
  • Providing evidence for needing response resources (e.g., requesting additional cooling center hours or water distribution sites)
  • Preparing emergency department personnel for visit surges during severe, prolonged heat waves

How was the data set created?

Derivation of measure

  1. Measures 1-3:

    Numerator:

    For data before 2016: Patients treated in an emergency department (ED) having any ICD-9 code in the range of 992.0 or cause of injury code E900.0 or E900.9, EXCLUDING cases with a code of E900.1 (man-made source of heat) anywhere in the record.

    For data 2016 to present: Patients treated in an emergency department (ED) having ICD-10 codes T67, X30, or X32, EXCLUDING cases with a code W92 not of natural origin anywhere in the record.

    Denominator:

    Midyear resident population, by sex, for state and by county

    Adjustment:

    • Age-adjustment by the direct method to the year 2000 U.S. standard population
    • U.S. 2000 standard population by age categories from Surveillance Epidemiology and End Results (SEER), National Cancer Institute

    Measures 4-5:

    Daily and weekly rates for each U.S. Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) region reflect heat-related illness (HRI)–associated emergency department (ED) visits per 100,000 ED visits during a specific day or week using data available through the National Syndromic Surveillance Program (NSSP) at the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC).

    Syndromic surveillance uses near real-time data from chief complaint terms and diagnosis codes routinely collected during healthcare visits. HRI is identified using a well-defined combination of keywords (based on reported symptoms and physician assessments that resemble heat stroke) and ICD codes associated with exposure to high ambient temperature (visit here for more information on the definition).

What are the limitations of the data set?

Measures 1-3:

Emergency department data:

  • Data are not available for all states.
  • Number of diagnostic fields in hospital records varies from state to state. Utilization of EDs varies geographically.

Census data:

  • Only available every 10 years, thus postcensal estimates are needed when calculating rates for years following the census year.

Measures 4-5:

  • National Syndromic Surveillance Program (NSSP) emergency department data are a convenience sample and should not be considered nationally representative. NSSP coverage has increased over time and varies by geographic region, and some emergency departments are not able to provide data back to the same point in history. To make year-over-year comparisons, we adjust for these differences in the daily and weekly rates to reduce artifactual impact from changes in reporting patterns by including only those emergency departments that consistently contributed data since 2018.
  • Data is updated daily. Rates may shift and take up to a few days for the most recent data to be stable.

How should these data be used?

Use Constraints

On October 1, 2015 in the United States, the International Classification of Diseases, Tenth Revision, Clinical Modification (ICD-10-CM) replaced the ninth revision (ICD-9-CM) for coding of medical terminology and disease classification. As a direct result of this change, there are nearly five times as many diagnosis codes in ICD-10-CM than in ICD-9-CM, allowing further expansion than was possible with ICD-9-CM. This coding change impacts information classifications for hospital discharge, emergency department, and outpatient records for administrative and financial transactions in all healthcare settings. Differences in counts and rates in years prior to 2015 (ICD-9-CM) compared with 2016 and subsequent years (ICD-10-CM) could be a result of this coding change and not an actual difference in the number of events.

Periods of extreme heat are frequently associated with increases in hospital visits and admissions for many causes. This measure does not capture the full spectrum of heat stress, where exposure to excess heat is not explicitly documented.
